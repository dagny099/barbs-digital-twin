# Asking Good Questions

The twin responds well to specific, context-rich questions — the same way Barbara herself would in a conversation. Here are question patterns that consistently produce strong answers.

---

## Question Patterns That Work Well

### Lead with a problem or goal

Instead of "What are Barbara's skills?", try:

> *"I'm evaluating Barbara for a role that involves building RAG systems and designing evaluation frameworks. What evidence do you have?"*

The twin is designed to answer *problems before skills* — leading with your actual goal gets a more targeted response.

### Ask for a project walkthrough

> *"Walk me through the Resume Explorer project — what problem it solves, how it works, and key design decisions."*

Featured projects have rich walkthrough context including architecture, tech stack, design insights, and live demo links where available. The twin will guide you through them.

### Explore connections

> *"How does Barbara's cognitive science background connect to her current work on RAG systems?"*

This is one of the most interesting things to ask. The connection between human attention and machine retrieval is something Barbara finds genuinely meaningful — and the twin is primed to explore it.

### Follow up

> *"Tell me more about the entity extraction piece."*
> *"Which of her projects is most relevant to a data engineering role?"*
> *"You mentioned ChromaDB — what's the relationship between that and Neo4j?"*

The twin maintains conversation context, so natural follow-ups work well.

---

## By Audience

=== "Recruiters & Hiring Managers"

    - *"What makes Barbara different from other data scientists?"*
    - *"Walk me through the Resume Explorer project"*
    - *"What's Barbara's experience with generative AI?"*
    - *"How does Barbara approach knowledge engineering?"*
    - *"What are Barbara's core technical skills?"*
    - *"What kind of role is Barbara looking for?"*

=== "Technical Collaborators"

    - *"What projects demonstrate Barbara's full-stack capabilities?"*
    - *"How does Barbara think about meaning-making in data work?"*
    - *"What's the connection between Barbara's cognitive science background and her current work?"*
    - *"Walk me through the Neo4j migration — what drove it and what were the tradeoffs?"*
    - *"How does the composite scoring formula work in her retrieval pipeline?"*

=== "Curious Visitors"

    - *"Tell me about yourself"*
    - *"What's your favorite project and why?"*
    - *"How did you transition from academia to independent AI work?"*
    - *"What influences your approach to data and technology?"*
    - *"What are you working on these days?"*
    - *"How does this chatbot actually work?"*

---

## What to Avoid

| Instead of... | Try... |
|---|---|
| *"List all your skills"* | *"What technical skills are most central to your current work?"* |
| *"What's your salary expectation?"* | Direct outreach — the twin will redirect this |
| *"Write me a cover letter"* | The twin is a portfolio assistant, not a writing tool |
| *"What happened on [specific date]?"* | The twin avoids dates unless clearly relevant (research, events) |
| *"Tell me everything about X"* | Specific angles get better answers than open-ended dumps |

---

## When the Twin Says "I Don't Know"

This is a feature. When the twin says something like *"That's not something I have in here yet"* or *"I have some context on X but not that specific detail"* — it's being honest, not failing.

You can ask the twin to flag the gap for Barbara:

> *"Can you let Barbara know I asked about X?"*

The twin will collect your name, email, and message and send a notification. No fabrication, no guessing — just a clean handoff.

!!! note
    The twin will never invent project outcomes, dates, company names, or biographical details. If the knowledge base doesn't have it, you'll hear "I don't know" — which is exactly what Barbara would say.
