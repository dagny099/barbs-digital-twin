# My Dissertation Research

## Document Type: Knowledge Base — Personal Narrative
## Topics: PhD, dissertation, research, MIT, cognitive science, visual search, eye tracking, attention, Aude Oliva, CVCL, labmates, grad school, committee, Jeremy Wolfe, George Alvarez, hybrid images, massive memory, teaching

My PhD dissertation is titled "Eye Movement Guidance in Familiar Visual Scenes: A Role for Scene Specific Location Priors in Search." I completed it at MIT in the Department of Brain and Cognitive Sciences in 2010, under the supervision of Aude Oliva.

## What I Studied

I studied how people's eyes move when they're searching for something in a scene they've seen before. Think about looking for your keys in your own kitchen versus someone else's kitchen — you're faster in yours, but why? My research investigated the specific mechanisms behind that advantage.

The core question was: when you've searched a scene before, does your memory of that specific scene guide where your eyes go next time? And if so, how quickly does that memory kick in?

## What I Found

Three main contributions came out of this work:

First, I established a benchmark for how consistently different people look at the same places when searching real-world scenes. We recorded 14 observers searching over 900 outdoor scenes for pedestrians and found remarkably high agreement in where people looked — even when the person they were searching for wasn't there. A computational model combining visual saliency, target features, and scene context predicted 94% of human agreement, with scene context being the strongest single predictor.

Second, I developed a new analytical method called Comparative Map Analysis. This technique lets you compare spatial patterns in eye movement data across different observers and different viewing sessions. Using this method across three separate experiments, I showed that your own past search history in a specific scene predicts where you'll look better than other people's history in the same scene. In other words, there's something uniquely informative about your personal search experience — it's not just general knowledge about "kitchens have counters."

Third, I designed a delayed-search paradigm to study the time course of memory retrieval during search. I found that giving people more time before they start moving their eyes in a familiar scene increases the probability that their very first eye movement goes directly to the target. This suggests that retrieving scene-specific memories takes time — it's not instantaneous — and that this retrieval process is what ultimately biases attention toward informative locations.

## The Practical Translation

My dissertation was fundamentally about how humans allocate attention efficiently using learned representations — how we build internal models of our environment and use them to guide behavior. Years later, I see echoes of these questions throughout AI and ML: how should a system direct limited computational resources toward the most informative parts of an input? How should prior experience shape that allocation? My research didn't answer those questions for machines, but it gave me a deep intuition for the underlying tradeoffs.

## Key Publications

The main published paper from this work is Ehinger, K.*, Hidalgo-Sotelo, B.*, Torralba, A., & Oliva, A. (2009). "Modeling Search for People in 900 Scenes: A combined source model of eye guidance." Visual Cognition, 17, 945-978. That paper has accumulated over 400 citations. Another key publication is Hidalgo-Sotelo, B. & Oliva, A. (2010). "Person, place, and past influence eye movements during visual search." 

An earlier conference paper — Hidalgo-Sotelo, B., Oliva, A., & Torralba, A. (2005). "Human Learning of Contextual Object Priors: Where does the time go?" — was presented at the IEEE Conference on Computer Vision and Pattern Recognition (CVPR) in San Diego. That was my first talk in grad school, and it was at a computer vision conference, which is notable because it planted my work squarely at the intersection of cognitive science and computational vision early on.


## My Advisor: Aude Oliva

Aude Oliva was my PhD advisor and has been a huge influence on how I think. She's now a Senior Research Scientist at MIT CSAIL, the MIT Director of the MIT-IBM Watson AI Lab, and Director of Strategic Industry Engagement in the MIT Schwarzman College of Computing. Her research spans human perception and cognition, computer vision, and cognitive neuroscience — she's one of the people who genuinely bridges those fields rather than just claiming to.

Aude is known for her work on scene recognition (the Places dataset, with 10 million annotated images, came from her lab), image memorability, and Hybrid Images — those famous images that look like one thing up close and another from far away (the classic example combines Einstein and Marilyn Monroe). She developed Hybrid Images with Philippe Schyns. She's also received a Guggenheim Fellowship, an NSF CAREER Award, and a Vannevar Bush Faculty Fellowship — she's the real deal. What I remember most, though, is that she was genuinely passionate about encouraging her students' curiosity and had a warmth that made the lab feel like a creative home.

## My Committee

My thesis committee included three leaders in visual cognition and attention research:

**Molly Potter** — a legendary figure in rapid visual processing at MIT. Her work on "rapid serial visual presentation" (RSVP) showed that the brain can extract meaning from images presented for as little as 100 milliseconds.

**Jeremy Wolfe** — Professor of Ophthalmology and Radiology at Harvard Medical School and Director of the Visual Attention Lab at Brigham & Women's Hospital. Jeremy is the creator of the Guided Search model, one of the most influential theoretical frameworks in visual attention research, now in its 6th version. He's also a Senior Lecturer in MIT's BCS department, where he teaches Introduction to Psychology. I was a TA for his Intro to Psychology course around 2006 — he's an incredible lecturer. His lab was just down the street from BCS, and it was a frequent place to give and listen to talks. The cross-pollination between CVCL and the Visual Attention Lab shaped a lot of my thinking, as well as personally encouraged me at critical points along my academic journey. I have heaps of gratitude for his support. 

**George Alvarez** — now the Fred Kavli Professor of Neuroscience and Professor of Psychology at Harvard, where he co-directs the Vision Sciences Laboratory with Talia Konkle. George's research focuses on how the visual system optimizes the use of its limited resources — attentional selection, memory storage, and efficient coding. He did a postdoc at MIT with Aude (2005-2008), overlapping with my time there, and was a key collaborator on the Massive Memory experiments with Tim Brady and Talia Konkle. George was on my committee and also a co-author on several papers that shaped the lab's direction. I learned a lot from George as a researcher and person. 

**Todd Horowitz** — while not on my committee, Todd was the Associate Director of the Visual Attention Lab alongside Jeremy Wolfe, and an important presence in that community. He's now a Program Director at the National Cancer Institute, where he applies visual attention research to medical image perception — studying how radiologists search for cancers in mammograms and how prevalence affects detection rates. His work on the "low prevalence effect" with Wolfe (showing that rare targets are missed more often) has had real clinical impact.

## Teaching at MIT

I co-taught a course on Visual Cognition with Aude Oliva in 2011 — an undergraduate course with a lab component. I was also head TA for Introduction to Psychology when it was taught by John Gabrieli (around 2007) and TA for the same course when taught by Jeremy Wolfe (around 2006). Teaching Intro Psych at MIT was a formative experience — it forced me to translate complex ideas into clear, accessible language, which is a skill I value in everything I build now.

## My Lab and My People

I was part of MIT's Computational Visual Cognition Lab (CVCL), led by Aude Oliva. My co-author on the 900 Scenes paper, Krista Ehinger, was a close collaborator — we shared first authorship. Antonio Torralba was a key collaborator on the computational modeling side, as well a great listener and mentor.

My labmates in CVCL were an incredible group of people who have gone on to remarkable careers:

**Talia Konkle** — now a Professor of Psychology at Harvard, Director of the Mind Brain Behavior Interfaculty Initiative, and Kempner Institute Associate Faculty. Talia's research explores how the brain organizes visual knowledge, from individual objects to scenes. At MIT, she did fascinating experiments on "visual framing" (having participants sketch objects on different sizes of paper to show that people naturally scale their drawings to match real-world object size) and collaborated with Tim, George, and Aude on the Massive Memory experiments and Hybrid Images research. She and George Alvarez now co-direct the VisionLab at Harvard together.

**Timothy Brady** — now a Professor of Psychology at UC San Diego, running the Vision and Memory Lab. Tim, along with Talia, George Alvarez, and Aude, ran the Massive Memory experiments — their 2008 PNAS paper showed that people could recall remarkably specific details about images they had only seen briefly, demonstrating that visual long-term memory has a massive storage capacity for object details. It's one of the most-cited papers to come out of our lab.

**Michelle Greene** — now an Assistant Professor of Psychology at Barnard College (Columbia University), with an NSF CAREER award. Michelle did behavioral and brain imaging studies showing how the visual system responds to scene-level properties — global properties described with a human frame of reference, like "Openness" and "Navigability." She recently published research uncovering socioeconomic biases in AI scene classification systems.

**Krista Ehinger** — my co-first-author on the 900 Scenes paper and friend whom I played Guitar Hero with.

**Olivier Joubert, Soojin Park** (now Assistant Professor of Cognitive Science at Johns Hopkins), and **Emmanuelle Boloix** (postdoc who became a close friend and housemate) were also part of CVCL.

Beyond the lab, I had an extraordinary set of friends in the BCS department: Dominique Pritchett (now Associate Professor of Biology at Howard University, doing neuroscience research with AI/ML), his wife Tracy Pritchett (PhD from Boston University), Jennifer Olejarczyk (lab assistant in Molly Potter's lab, close friend and housemate), Ed Vul (now at UC San Diego/Amazon), Todd Thompson, Ethan Meyers, Reuben Goodman, John Kramer, Retsina Meyer, Vikash Mansinghka, and Jim Munch. Grad school is hard, and these people made it not just survivable but genuinely fun.
