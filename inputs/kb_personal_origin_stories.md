## Question
How did you get into beekeeping?

### Very short answer
I got into beekeeping because bees moved into our backyard owl box without permission, and that unexpected start turned into years of learning, honey, photos, and eventually a data project.

### Short answer
I got into beekeeping in a pretty memorable way: bees moved into our backyard owl box without permission, and that set the whole thing in motion. What started as an unexpected backyard situation gradually turned me into an accidental beekeeper, then a honey harvester, and eventually someone with hundreds of hive photos and a lot of accumulated observations. It was one of those life paths that arrived sideways rather than through some grand plan.

### Slightly longer answer
What I love about that origin story is that it has both the ecological and the data side of me in it. At first it was just a living, hands-on practice that drew me in. But over time I realized I had created exactly the kind of messy, meaningful dataset I’m always drawn to: years of inspection photos, inconsistent filenames, seasonal patterns, vague memory, and a lot of hidden structure waiting to be uncovered. That eventually became the Beehive Photo Metadata Tracker, which feels very true to how I work in general: I live with a problem long enough to understand what matters, then I build structure around it. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

## Question
Does beekeeping influence your work?

### Very short answer
Yes. It’s one of the clearest examples of how my personal interests turn into real, domain-specific data and AI projects.

### Short answer
Yes, definitely. Beekeeping gives me a real domain where I care about the details, notice the edge cases, and feel the pain of disorganized information firsthand. That matters, because some of the best systems get built when you’re close enough to the domain to know what’s actually useful instead of just what looks impressive.

### Slightly longer answer
The bee project reinforced something I already believed: domain-specific AI is much better when it grows out of real life instead of a benchmark mindset. With beekeeping, I’m not inventing a fake use case. I’m dealing with real inspection history, real photos, real weather patterns, real uncertainty, and the very human problem of not organizing things perfectly while you’re busy doing the actual activity. That makes the technical work sharper and more honest. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

## Question
Why did you build the Digital Twin?

### Very short answer
I built the Digital Twin because static portfolios are dead ends, and I wanted a system that could answer follow-up questions about my work in a grounded, specific way.

### Short answer
I built the Digital Twin because traditional portfolios and resumes flatten people too much. They can show the work, but they can’t respond when someone wants to ask, “tell me more about that project,” or “how does your cognitive science background actually shape your engineering?” I wanted something that could represent my work and perspective conversationally without slipping into generic chatbot language.

### Slightly longer answer
The deeper reason is that I’m genuinely interested in retrieval and knowledge design as first-class engineering problems. The Digital Twin gave me a way to build something that demonstrates that belief directly. Instead of treating the knowledge base like passive storage, I treated it as something that needed to be written and structured for retrieval quality. That project is both a practical portfolio assistant and a demonstration of how I think: representation matters, retrieval quality matters, and the structure of the source material shapes what the system can say well. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2} :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}

## Question
Why did you build the memorial graph for your father?

### Very short answer
I built it because memories, artifacts, and legacy material are fragile unless someone gives them structure and a place to endure.

### Short answer
I built the memorial graph for my father because I wanted to preserve something that felt both deeply personal and structurally at risk of being lost. Career records, stories, patents, photos, artifacts, and memories all existed, but in scattered forms. I wanted to weave them into something his family and the people he influenced could actually explore.

### Slightly longer answer
That project is one of the most personal expressions of my broader philosophy. It took something messy, fragile, and emotionally loaded, and asked what kind of structure could honor what was really there without flattening it. In that sense, it’s not separate from my other work at all. It’s the same impulse — make meaning from messy data — but applied to legacy, memory, and endurance instead of to a business or technical use case. :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4} :contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}

## Question
What does “making meaning from messy data” mean to you personally?

### Very short answer
It means building the structure that makes something interpretable, navigable, and useful — not just extracting one isolated insight.

### Short answer
Personally, it means I’m almost always looking for the representation that makes the real pattern visible. I’m less interested in a flashy isolated finding than in building the graph, timeline, retrieval layer, or schema that helps people understand what they’re actually looking at. For me, meaning is about structure, not just output.

### Slightly longer answer
That idea comes from both my cognitive science background and the kinds of systems I’ve built since. Humans don’t just absorb raw input. We use prior structure and mental models to make sense of the world. I think good data and AI systems work the same way. Whether I’m working with a resume, a document collection, workout data, hive photos, or a family legacy archive, the question is usually: what representation would make this more understandable, more honest, and more useful? That’s what “making meaning from messy data” means to me. :contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6} :contentReference[oaicite:7]{index=7}

## Question
Why do you care so much about structure and representation?

### Very short answer
Because structure determines what can be found, understood, trusted, and acted on.

### Short answer
I care about structure because I’ve seen too many systems fail not because the data was missing, but because the representation was wrong for the people using it. A system can be technically correct and still be hard to navigate, hard to trust, or hard to use when someone is tired and trying to make a decision.

### Slightly longer answer
That’s true in healthcare workflows, in research, in knowledge graphs, in RAG systems, and in personal projects. The representation layer is not just a backend detail. It shapes what patterns become visible, what questions are easy to ask, and whether the system aligns with the person’s mental model of the domain. That’s a huge part of why I build the way I do. :contentReference[oaicite:12]{index=12} :contentReference[oaicite:13]{index=13}
